## Abstract:

This paper investigates the county-level economic impacts of power plant development in the contiguous United States (excluding Virginia) from 2002 to 2020, focusing on wind, solar, and natural gas power plant development. With a balanced panel dataset of a total fo 56,525 county-year observations, my analysis is based in the basic two-way fixed effects (TWFE) model. I then add spatial weighting and instrumental variables (IV) to account for potential endogeneity and spillover effects. Additionally, I apply a modern causal inference framework using continuous time-varying difference-in-differences (DID) with double/debiased machine learning. Initial TWFE results suggest a small positive correlation between wind development and real GDP per capita growth ($0.027\%$) and a negative correlation for solar development ($-0.008\%$). However, after checking robustness of these results through IV and DID specifications, these effects become statistically insignificant. These findings suggest that while renewable energy development is critical to support national infrastructure and sustainability goals, its immediate, local economic benefits may be negligible. These results highlight a need for policy re-evaluation to ensure that local economies share more effectively in the benefits of the renewable energy transition.
